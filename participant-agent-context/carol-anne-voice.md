# Carol Anne's Voice — Quotes by Theme

Direct quotes from Carol Anne Hilton's two books, extracted from the published worldview JSONs in the IndigenomicsAI repository. Every quote is reproduced verbatim from the `source_quote` field of the extraction. Page numbers are preserved where the extraction captured them.

**Book 1** — *Indigenomics: Taking a Seat at the Economic Table* (2021).
**Book 2** — *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power* (2024).

**Discipline:** quote Carol Anne directly. Do not paraphrase her framings into new wording. If you need a framing that is not here, the answer is to ask her, not to invent one.

## Book 1 — Ten Principles (foundational worldview)

These ten principles are extracted from *Indigenomics: Taking a Seat at the Economic Table* (Book 1). Only Principles 2 and 3 carry a direct key quote in the published extraction; the rest are summarised by Carol Anne's editorial team. The summaries are not direct quotes and are not marked as such.

### Principle 1 — Everything Is Connected
*Nuu-chah-nulth:* hish uk ish tsa walk — "Everything is one and interconnected"

*Essence (editorial summary, not a direct quote):* Relational economics — the economic construct of relativity. All living things form part of an integrated whole brought into harmony through mutual respect.

### Principle 2 — Story
> "All that we are is story. From the moment we are born to the time we continue on our spirit journey, we are involved in the creation of the story of our time here." — Carol Anne Hilton, *Indigenomics: Taking a Seat at the Economic Table*, p. 65.

### Principle 3 — Animate Life Force
> "What you people call your natural resources our people call our relatives." — Carol Anne Hilton, *Indigenomics: Taking a Seat at the Economic Table*, p. 69.

### Principle 4 — Transformation
*Essence (editorial summary, not a direct quote):* The changing of form. Shape shifting between worlds, dimensions, physical and spiritual. Challenges assumptions about the nature of reality. Things are constantly undergoing processes of transformation, deformation and restoration.

### Principle 5 — The Teachings
*Essence (editorial summary, not a direct quote):* Original instructions — the responsibilities and ethics at the heart of Indigenous identity. Languages hold within them how to be, how to conduct oneself. Proximity to teachings reflects quality of life.

### Principle 6 — Creation Story
*Essence (editorial summary, not a direct quote):* Connection to origin and Creation is foundational. Creation stories define relationship to place, set requirements for how to be, demonstrate place-based values including resource management and governance systems.

### Principle 7 — Protocol
*Essence (editorial summary, not a direct quote):* The highest form of expression of recognition. The responsibility of remembering, acknowledging, and witnessing. Right relations as a core premise. Thousands of years of forming and confirming relationships.

### Principle 8 — To Witness
*Essence (editorial summary, not a direct quote):* The sacred responsibility of remembering. Witnesses validate experience, agreements, ceremony across time. Knowledge was the most valued of all possessions. Relationship as transactional across time.

### Principle 9 — To Make Visible
*Essence (editorial summary, not a direct quote):* The limitations of human understanding of reality. Duality of spiritual and physical reality. What is visible and what is invisible — what we experience on Earth is only part of the whole dimension.

### Principle 10 — Renewal
*Essence (editorial summary, not a direct quote):* Shedding the old, being newborn, transition from one state to another. Ceremony is embedded with renewal — reinforcing commitments, protocols, and generational relationships.

## Book 2 — Quotes by Theme

### Recovery from the Indian Act

**Theme — Government Policies, Agreements, and Reconciliation Arrangements**

> "Impediments to Indigenous economic development—put in place by the Indian Act, which effectively took Indigenous communities out of the mainstream economy—have held Indigenous Peoples back for generations." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 8, 9.

> "The Indian Act of 1867 has never reflected the spirit and intent of the Royal Proclamation of 1763, which described a sharing of resources and a respect for the existing governance of Indigenous communities." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 10.

> "The Indigenous socioeconomic gap can be viewed through this experience of 'losing influence over our own lives' as Indigenous Peoples. This is the Indigenous socioeconomic gap—the absence of belonging. This is Indian Act economics." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 27, 28, 65, 66.

**Theme — UNDRIP Implementation into Law**

> "Like so many others, I have spent my years recovering; recovering from the effects of the Indian Act and recovering from a generational response to economic displacement as an Indigenous person. It is from here that I draw both my response and inspiration." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 126, 7.

> "Aligning the Indian Act to be in accordance with UNDRIP involves legislative changes and a broader shift in the relationship between the Canadian government, regional governments, and Indigenous Nations that move toward true partnership and respect for Indigenous sovereignty, rights, and self-determination" — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 223.

> "We are only now beginning to discuss the fundamental rights of Indigenous peoples as human rights... it is important that we remind ourselves that the Indigenous Peoples' fundamental rights in this country are indeed human rights." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 224.
