# 25-Theme Ontology — Summary for Spec Drafting

The 25 themes of the Indigenomics ontology v1.0, extracted from Chapter 8 of *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*. The ontology answers Carol Anne's question: *how is value creation happening in the Indigenous economy?* It is a classification instrument, not a tag list — every spec idea, claim, or evidence record can land in one or more themes.

**For each theme** below:
- **Definition** — drawn from the published ontology (Carol Anne's definitional framing).
- **Anchoring quote** — a direct quote from Carol Anne where the extraction captured one.
- **Sample build-spec ideas** — non-exhaustive starter ideas for participant teams. These are illustrations, not directives; teams should generate their own.

**Layer notes:** *Layer A* themes are partially captured by Canada's existing IPEA (Indigenous People's Economic Account); *Layer B* themes are excluded from IPEA and represent the expansion opportunity. The ontology is GDP-departing — it asks where value is being created *by* the Indigenous economy, not what the Indigenous economy contributes to national totals.

## 1. Land Transfer

**Definition.** The growing estimate of Indigenous-controlled or -owned lands is a significant development in the Canadian and the Indigenous economy. Indigenous land transfers are facilitated through specific claims or settlement cases that address historic wrongs against Indigenous Peoples and are catalyzing economic growth.

**Phase 3 framing.** Land repatriation drives wealth, leveragability and stewardship.

**Layer:** B  ·  **GDP coverage:** excluded  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 154-157

**Anchoring quote.**

> "The lands we collectively live, work, and play on hold the vibration of the original treaties, covering over 3.5 million square kilometers or about 35 percent of the country's total land mass. This is the common invisible thread across our collective reality." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 15, 16.

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- A tracker that visualizes parcel-level land transfers to Nations over time, surfacing the gap between mapped Crown land and Nation-controlled territory.
- A briefing tool that drafts a public-readable explainer for any specific-claim settlement, linked back to underlying treaty obligations.

## 2. Indigenous Funds

**Definition.** Increasingly, Indigenous Nations and businesses are identifying the space and structure for Indigenous-led capital. This development provides a significant opportunity for financial partners to create collaborative structures that advance investment accessibility, alignment, and economic reconciliation outcomes.

**Phase 3 framing.** Indigenous-led capital and investment vehicles.

**Layer:** B  ·  **GDP coverage:** excluded  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 157-158

**Anchoring quote.**

> "every dollar invested is linked explicitly to improved outcomes and well-being for Indigenous Peoples" — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 169, 263.

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- A directory + simple filter of Indigenous-led capital vehicles by region and investment focus, designed for Nation finance officers.
- A back-of-envelope sizing tool for an Indigenous-led fund: capital base, deployment rate, expected return, governance structure.

## 3. Indigenous Legal Developments

**Definition.** The tension between the advancement of Indigenous Rights and the advancement of Indigenous economic space in the Canadian economy is constantly being expressed. Court rulings (Haida, Tsilhqot'in, Mi'kmaw treaty rights) expand rights-based economic activities.

**Phase 3 framing.** Court rulings expand rights-based economic activities and regulatory adjustment.

**Layer:** B  ·  **GDP coverage:** excluded  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 158-160

**Anchoring quote.**

> "The Indian Act is an architectural structure designed specifically for the absence of belonging of Indigenous Peoples and their dis-invitation from having a seat at the economic table" — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 24.

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- A timeline of landmark court rulings that lets you trace which subsequent economic outcomes each ruling enabled (Tsilhqot'in, Haida, Mi'kmaw treaty rights).
- A 'jurisprudence-as-interface' prototype: trace any amendment → commitment-formation → witnessed-outcome as a single navigable civic flow.

## 4. Increasing Indigenous Project and Partnership Values

**Definition.** A key trend in Indigenous economic empowerment is the increasing project values of Indigenous business partnerships. Partnerships are foundational to creating outcomes in Indigenous economic reconciliation. At least 38 Canadian energy projects worth $27B+ feature Indigenous equity ownership.

**Phase 3 framing.** Co-ownership and partnership development in economic projects.

**Layer:** A+B  ·  **GDP coverage:** partially_captured  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 160-162

**Anchoring quote.**

> "Ensure that financial agreements include provisions for equitable benefit sharing with Indigenous Nations and businesses. This might involve developing profit-sharing, community development funds, or other financial arrangements." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 231, 232.

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- A scorecard for a proposed partnership: equity %, governance seats, benefit-sharing structure, exit terms, ongoing review cadence.
- A pattern library of Indigenous-equity energy partnerships ($27B+ in projects), browsable by sector and Nation.

## 5. Infrastructure Development — Major Projects

**Definition.** The First Nations Major Projects Coalition (FNMPC) supports Indigenous Nations in participating in major infrastructure and resource projects across Canada. Focus on equity ownership, environmental stewardship, and governance over large-scale developments.

**Phase 3 framing.** Indigenous ownership in large-scale developments.

**Layer:** A+B  ·  **GDP coverage:** partially_captured  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 162-163

**Anchoring quote.**

> "This is it. This is what the emerging $100 billion Indigenous economy looks like. A collective series of Indigenous economic development projects connected together in an investment environment that is responding to the growth of the Indigenous economy." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 139.

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- A regional dashboard showing all major infrastructure projects with mapped Indigenous equity positions and outstanding consultation status.
- A loan-guarantee impact estimator: given a federal guarantee program, project the equity participation it would unlock.

## 6. Indigenous Trade

**Definition.** Indigenous trade structures and agreements are modern tools advancing the Indigenous economy. Canada's Trade Diversification Strategy includes Indigenous Peoples in international trade and investment agreements. The IPETCA is a collaborative initiative advancing Indigenous economic participation in international trade.

**Phase 3 framing.** Indigenous businesses and Nations shaping and advancing global trade agreements and activity.

**Layer:** A+B  ·  **GDP coverage:** partially_captured  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 163-165

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- An export-readiness checklist for a Nation-owned enterprise considering IPETCA-supported market entry.
- A simple matchmaking surface between Indigenous exporters and Indigenous-friendly importers in IPETCA member economies.

## 7. Capital Alignment

**Definition.** Shift from the singular narrative of 'capital access as barrier' toward positive action examining the alignment of capital to the growth of the Indigenous economy. Finance aligned with Indigenous values and economic participation.

**Phase 3 framing.** Finance aligned with Indigenous values. Corporate leadership increasing access to capital.

**Layer:** B  ·  **GDP coverage:** excluded  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 165-166

**Anchoring quote.**

## 8. Procurement

**Definition.** Indigenous procurement has rapidly ascended as a core economic enabler. Policy space for Indigenous procurement as an economic design tool for government and industry is a significant advancement. Example: Article 24 of the Nunavut Settlement Agreement mandates Government of Canada to support Inuit businesses through procurement.

**Phase 3 framing.** Framework to increase Indigenous-owned business participation in government and corporate supply chains — an economic and reconciliation tool shifting who benefits from public and private spending.

**Layer:** A+B  ·  **GDP coverage:** partially_captured  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 166-168

**Anchoring quote.**

> "This mandate signals a proactive approach to advancing Indigenous economic participation and build reconciliation outcomes. It is a key trend to watch in the rise of Indigenous economic power." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 179, 180.

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- A federal procurement watchlist: contracts above $X with Indigenous procurement targets, with current set-aside fulfillment status.
- A Nation business-readiness checklist for federal supplier registration and Article 24 (Nunavut) qualification.

## 9. Indigenous Assets Under Management

**Phase 3 framing.** Growing Nation-owned portfolios — investments, land holdings, trusts, and equity positions owned, managed, or governed by Indigenous Nations and institutions.

**Layer:** B  ·  **GDP coverage:** excluded  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 168-170

**Definition.** Indigenous Peoples are creating new businesses at five times the rate of non-Indigenous peoples. Statistics Canada reports nearly 19,000 businesses in Indigenous communities (~17,000 in First Nations, ~2,000 in Inuit). The rise of Indigenous entrepreneurship is a powerful phenomenon.

**Phase 3 framing.** Indigenous business creation outpaces national averages.

**Layer:** A+B  ·  **GDP coverage:** partially_captured  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 170-173

**Anchoring quote.**

> "of isolated islands but a vast interconnected ecosystem. Every decision we make, every partnership we form, and every project we embark upon is evaluated through the lens of this deep interconnectedness." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 152, 153.

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- A founder-onboarding flow that surfaces Indigenous-focused capital, mentorship networks, and procurement opportunities relevant to the founder's sector.
- A Salish-Sea ecological economy explainer: how Indigenous-owned aquaculture, kelp, and forestry enterprises participate in regional supply chains.

## 11. Indigenous Trusts

**Definition.** Trust structures commonly used to safeguard assets for future generations. Connected to both settlement process and asset management. The quickening pace of Indigenous trust development has made this a highly competitive arena with financial partners.

**Phase 3 framing.** Judicial Settlement Trusts protect and manage settlement funds with transparent governance. Wealth Management Trusts invest and grow Nation-owned capital to generate income and build intergenerational prosperity.

**Layer:** B  ·  **GDP coverage:** excluded  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 173-175

**Anchoring quote.**

> "The pathways for the increasing pace of Indigenous trust developments are based on Nations challenging historical injustices, which is creating new economic space for Nations through financial leverage and increasing influ- ence within the financial sector" — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 186.

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- A trust-design comparator: settlement trust vs wealth trust vs hybrid, with governance and tax-treatment trade-offs.
- A drafter for a Nation trust-purpose statement that grounds asset deployment in Nation priorities.

## 12. Indigenous Sovereign Wealth Funds

**Definition.** Sovereign wealth funds invest state-earned revenues; created through legislation within a sovereign government. This financial mechanism is being adapted by Indigenous Nations as an increasing trend. What makes it 'sovereign' is that it is created through an act of legislation within a sovereign government.

**Phase 3 framing.** Nation-led long-term financial planning — investing collective wealth aligned with cultural values to sustain self-determined growth and secure prosperity for future generations.

**Layer:** B  ·  **GDP coverage:** excluded  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 175-177

**Anchoring quote.**

> "The development of Indigenous sovereign wealth is a trend to pay attention to." — Carol Anne Hilton, *The Rise of Indigenous Economic Power*, p. 187.

**Sample build-spec ideas.**

- A peer-comparison view across early-stage Indigenous sovereign wealth funds (KSWF and others), governance and disbursement policies side by side.
- A simple SWF stand-up checklist: enabling legislation, asset-mix policy, governance board, distribution rules.

## 25. Urban Reserves

**Definition.** An Indigenous urban reserve is similar to a municipal property but is a section of reserve land within a larger municipality. Urban reserves are created to stimulate business activity, generate revenue, and create investment and employment opportunities otherwise unavailable on other reserve lands. 'First Nations economic zones.'

**Phase 3 framing.** Nations establishing reserve lands within cities to drive business development, investment, and employment — expanding Nation-owned assets and creating spaces of economic sovereignty within Canada's urban centres.

**Layer:** B  ·  **GDP coverage:** excluded  ·  **Book 2 reference:** Chapter 8, p. 195-197

**Anchoring quote.**

## Cross-cutting notes

**Theme relationships matter.** The ontology specifies how themes feed into each other, overlap, depend on, or hold tension with each other. A spec touching one theme often actually touches three or four; check the JSON-LD bundle for the relationship graph.

**Anti-glossary lists matter.** Each theme has an `anti_glossary` of related terms that should redirect elsewhere (e.g., "fee simple purchase" is theme 22, not theme 1; "procurement" is theme 8, not theme 4 even though both involve money flowing to Indigenous businesses). Use these to keep spec scope clean.

**Layer A vs Layer B.** Layer A themes (Partnerships, Major Projects, Trade, Procurement, Entrepreneurship, Clean Energy, Equity Participation) are partially measured by IPEA. The other 18 are Layer B — invisible to the existing measurement apparatus. A spec that surfaces Layer B value is doing something the dominant economic vocabulary literally cannot see.
